Act I — Childhood
Image © LEDERER Timepieces
Most discussions about watchmaking focus on answers.
The answers are visible: A new mechanism. A technical solution. An invention. A result.
What is usually forgotten are the questions that produced them.
Every important development in watchmaking begins as a problem, a limitation, or an observation. Someone notices that a solution could be improved, challenged, or approached differently. The answer eventually becomes part of history. The question that created it is often forgotten.
The first chapter of 40 Moments with Bernhard begins before the answers.
It begins with childhood.
Not because childhood itself is the story, but because it provides the earliest visible traces of a pattern that would later remain remarkably consistent throughout decades of work.

A POCKET WATCH: The earliest memory discussed in this chapter is a pocket watch that belonged to Bernhard's grandfather.
The watch itself is not important because of its rarity or technical significance. What matters is that it introduced a simple but powerful idea: mechanical objects contain explanations waiting to be discovered. The watch was no longer simply an object that displayed time. It became something that raised questions.
The grandfather's pocket watch recalled in Act I.
BOOKS: Questions naturally led to books.
Books provided access to ideas, mechanisms, and solutions developed by people separated by decades or even centuries. More importantly, they revealed that similar problems could often be approached in different ways. One solution did not automatically exclude another.
The existence of alternatives became increasingly interesting.
FLEA MARKETS: Books eventually led beyond books.
Visits to flea markets created opportunities to encounter old clocks, watches, and mechanical objects directly. Instead of seeing mechanisms through illustrations or descriptions, they could now be examined physically. Different generations of makers had often attempted to solve similar problems, yet their solutions frequently differed.
The objects themselves became evidence of different ways of thinking.
MECHANICAL SYSTEMS: The same pattern appeared elsewhere.
Mechanical principles were not confined to watches and clocks. Similar ideas could be found in entirely different systems and machines. Looking closely enough revealed recurring relationships between problems and solutions.
The realization was important because it shifted attention away from individual objects and toward the logic behind them.
Different Answers
Taken individually, none of these experiences appears especially significant.
Together, however, they pointed toward the same conclusion.
A problem could have more than one answer.
An accepted solution could coexist with an alternative solution.
A mechanism could function successfully without being the only possible approach.
The idea would later become central to Bernhard's work, but its origins can already be seen here.
Living With the Questions
Eventually, the search for answers became active rather than passive.
One example discussed in this chapter is a journey to London in search of a watchmaking book that promised a deeper understanding. The distance itself is less important than what it reveals. By this point, questions were no longer simply appearing. They were being pursued.
The same period also reveals something else. As Bernhard's interest in horology deepened, watches and clocks gradually became part of everyday life. During his apprenticeship years, repair work often continued at home, eventually filling his apartment with ticking timepieces awaiting attention.
In the accompanying anecdote, he recalls the first time his parents came to stay with him and discovered that spending a night surrounded by dozens of clocks was not quite as peaceful as expected.
